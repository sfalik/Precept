Five Channels

Alternative palette exploration — one unmistakable hue per DSL category  ·  April 2026

1. The Idea

What if every DSL category owned a completely distinct hue?

The locked palette puts 4 of 6 families in the 195°–245° blue band. States, Events, Data, and Structure are all "some shade of blue." The separation is real in a swatch grid but subtle when scanning code at speed.

Five Channels gives each category its own unmistakable hue — no two families share a color family. You can identify any token's role from across the room.

Key departures from the locked palette:
  • Structure goes achromatic — no hue at all. Keywords are grammar, not content.
  • Fields go warm copper — the biggest departure. Data is material, grounded, earthy.
  • States go teal — 160°, far from events at 215°. Clear noun/verb separation.
  • Events stay blue but own it exclusively — the ONLY blue on the page.
  • Transitions get violet — -> and transition have their own voice, marking cause/effect seams.
  • Rules stay gold — the one thing that already works perfectly.

6. Analysis — Tradeoffs

Advantages

  • Category identification at a glance. Every DSL category has its own hue — no two families can be confused. You can identify token roles from across a room.
  • Warm data stands out. Copper field names pop against the dark background. In the locked palette, slate field names deliberately recede — which is a design choice, but "receding" can also mean "invisible."
  • Violet arrows create visual rhythm. Pipeline stages (->) get their own color, making the cause-effect chain scannable: violet, gray, copper, violet, violet+teal.
  • Structure truly disappears. Achromatic keywords can't compete with any semantic token — they're grammar, not content.
  • Noun/verb separation is unambiguous. Teal states vs blue events — 55° apart instead of 25°.

Concerns

  • Copper ↔ Gold proximity. Data (25°) and Rules (45°) are only 20° apart. Field names in copper and because in gold could blend on some monitors. The locked palette has the same concern (Data 215° ↔ Azure 220° = 5°) but those are both intentionally cool and quiet.
  • More visual noise. The locked palette is deliberately monotone — indigo/azure/sky/slate form a cohesive blue wash that lets you focus on reading. Five Channels is higher-contrast and busier — great for scanning, potentially tiring for long reading.
  • Achromatic structure loses brand identity. In the locked palette, indigo keywords ARE the brand — precept in #4338CA is the brand color. Gray keywords are generic. The brand lives nowhere in the syntax.
  • Warm copper is unconventional. No major editor theme uses warm tones for variable names. It could feel foreign to developers' muscle memory.
  • More colors = harder theme maintenance. 6 distinct hue families means more work to produce a coherent light-mode variant.
The core question: The locked palette optimizes for cohesion — everything feels like it belongs to one family. Five Channels optimizes for discrimination — everything is instantly identifiable. These are opposing priorities. Which matters more for a DSL that people read as a complete document (cohesion) vs scan for specific tokens (discrimination)?

18.2 — Semantic Alignment: Do the Colors Mean the Right Things?

Structure → Indigo (239–245°)

Indigo is architectural, authoritative, foundational. In fashion and design, deep blue signals "institutional" — the color of blueprints, technical drawings, corporate identity. Perfect for DSL scaffolding: precept, field, state, event, from, on. These tokens define what the machine is. Their low luminance (CR 2.5–4.4) also creates a natural "recedes into architecture" effect — you don't read them, you sense them framing the content.

✓ Strong semantic fit. No change recommended.

Data → Bright Slate + Italic (215°)

Slate is neutral, documentary, "ink on paper." Data tokens are the domain content — field names like TimeLeft, Minutes, type names like number, literal values like 0. Neutrality works: data is "the facts" — it shouldn't editorialize with a strong hue. The italic adds a humanistic quality: this is prose about the domain, not machine keywords. It also matches most developers' mental model — variables in VS Code are typically light/neutral. The 3-tier luminance within data (names brightest, types mid, values dimmest) mirrors information priority: names are what you look for, types are confirmatory, values are detail.

✓ Good semantic fit. The brightness upgrade from locked palette (#94A3B8 → #B0BEC5) makes data "present" without making it "loud."
⚠ Minor concern: slate at 215° is only 24° from indigo structure at 239°. But the italic + luminance gap (CR 8 data vs CR 2.5 structure) compensates fully. No one will confuse TimeLeft with field.

States → Violet (260°)

States are the anchors of the state machine — the places that exist, the nodes in the graph. Violet (purple) carries associations with significance, permanence, and landmark status. Historically, purple was the color of royalty because it was rare and stable — fitting for states that are the fixed, defined waypoints of a system. Violet also sits at the perceptual "warm edge" of the blue family, giving states a subtle warmth that makes them feel like places you arrive at rather than things that flow past you (which events should feel like).

✓ Strong semantic fit. The shift from locked azure (220°) to violet (260°) was the pivotal improvement — it gives states their own identity.
⚠ Proximity to indigo structure (21°). Diagnosed above — luminance gap (CR 7 vs CR 2.5) makes this safe in practice. But test with CVD simulators (below).

18.4 — Color Vision Deficiency (CVD) Analysis

~8% of males and ~0.5% of females have some form of CVD. How does this palette hold up?

Protanopia / Deuteranopia (red-green, ~6% of males)

Red-green CVD collapses the hue wheel to a blue-yellow axis. Under simulation:

  • Gold rules → seen as yellow. ✓ Still warm, still the standout.
  • Cyan events → seen as blue. ✓ Still bright, still distinct from gold.
  • Violet states → seen as blue. ⚠ May merge with indigo structure. But structure is CR ~2.5 dim, states are CR ~7.0+ bright. Luminance saves this.
  • Slate data → seen as blue-gray. ⚠ May merge with violet states. But data is italic, states are normal. Typography saves this.
  • Verdict green (#34D399) vs verdict red (#F87171) → both seen as dark yellow/brown. This is a known problem with traffic-light palettes under protanopia. Consider adding shape cues (✓/✕ icons) in verdict rendering.

Tritanopia (blue-yellow, ~0.003%)

Extremely rare. Collapses blue vs green. Violet and cyan could merge. Gold rules shift pinkish. Structure and states would need luminance and typography to separate. Given the rarity, this is acceptable but worth documenting.

Overall CVD assessment: The palette is safe for red-green CVD because every hue-adjacent pair has at least one non-hue differentiator (luminance, typography, or weight). The only true CVD risk is in runtime verdict colors (green/red), which is a presentation-layer concern, not a DSL editing concern.

Verdict: Should define before shipping. 3-tier state visibility is part of the DSL's semantic model.

3. Stationary events — do they need a separate shade?

The locked palette had Transition events (#3AC8F8), Conditional events (#7DDAFC), and Stationary events (#0FB0EB). The candidate uses a single event color (#30B8E8). If the language server distinguishes these subtypes, consider two or three cyan shades. However, event subtypes are determined by usage context (which transition rows they appear in), not by intrinsic declaration — so a uniform event color may actually be more honest. The event name doesn't change meaning; the transition row using it does.

Verdict: Single event color is likely the right call. Event subtype is a contextual property, not an intrinsic one.

4. String literals vs rule messages

Currently, because "message" uses gold #FBBF24 for the string literal. But the DSL also has regular string defaults: field Name as string default "unknown". Should "unknown" be gold or italic slate? Gold feels wrong for a data default — it's not a rule. Slate italic (#84929F) is technically correct but may look odd for a string literal. Consider: string literals that appear after because/assert get gold; string literals that appear as defaults get data-values color.

Verdict: This is a language server semantic token concern, not a palette concern. The palette already has the right colors — the question is which tokens get which color. Worth checking current behavior.

18.6 — Overall Assessment

Strengths

  • Gold isolation: The only warm token. Unmissable. Perfect for the DSL's "law" concept.
  • Violet states: The shift from azure (220°) to violet (260°) was the key unlock. States no longer recede into the blue middle.
  • Three-channel separation: Hue + luminance + typography means every pair of adjacent categories has at least two differentiators, not just one.
  • Semantic meaning matches perceptual behavior: Structure fades (scaffolding), data is warm-neutral prose (italic content), states are rich landmarks (violet anchors), events are active triggers (electric cyan), rules are bright warnings (gold alerts).
  • Brand identity: Indigo structure preserves the Precept brand color at the structural level where it's most visible (every precept starts with precept).

Remaining risks

  • Cool-side clustering: 65° of hue space holds 4 families. Acceptable with compensating cues, but monitor-dependent.
  • Structure at CR ~2.5: On bright monitors or non-calibrated displays, structure tokens may wash out against the #0c0c0f background. Test on multiple displays.

  • Verdict colors under CVD: Green/red verdict distinction fails for protanopia. Needs shape cues (icons/labels) in the preview panel.

Bottom line: This palette is well-designed and semantically coherent. The hue distribution is asymmetric (4 cool families, 1 warm), which is a structural limit — but the triple-channel approach (hue + luminance + typography) compensates effectively. The two concrete improvements worth making: (1) define the Final state tier (#C0B4F8), and (2) consider brightening rule keywords from #B8860B to ~#D4A017. Everything else is solid.
